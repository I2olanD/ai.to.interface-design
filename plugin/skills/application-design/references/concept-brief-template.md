# Concept Brief Template

Use this as the structure for the brief and position document — the output of Stages 1 and 2.

This is a **deliverable**, not a worksheet. The user should be able to read the finished brief and recognize their product in it. If they read it and shrug, the brief isn't done.

Fill it out in prose, not bullet points where prose belongs. Bullet points hide thinking. Prose exposes it.

---

## Template

```markdown
# [Project Name] — Concept Brief

## What this is

[One or two sentences. Function and scope. Don't editorialize yet.]

Example: "A meditation app for people who already meditate and find consumer wellness apps condescending. Mobile-first, single-session-focused, no streaks or social features."

## Who it's for

[A specific person in a specific situation. Avoid demographic language; reach for situation, mood, and motive.]

Example: "Someone in their 30s who has been meditating for years, doesn't want to be coached, and is tired of being told to 'smile and breathe.' They want a tool, not a teacher."

## When and where they'll use it

[The concrete moment of use. Time of day, location, mental state, what just happened, what's about to happen.]

Example: "Late evening, on the bedroom floor, after the kids are asleep. They've had a long day. They want twenty minutes of quiet. The phone screen will be the brightest thing in the room."

## What they're feeling

[One word for the entry state. One word for the desired exit state.]

Example: "Entry: depleted. Exit: settled."

## What this replaces

[The competing alternatives. Not just other apps — books, friends, silence, alcohol, scrolling.]

Example: "Replaces: scrolling Instagram, watching one more episode, a glass of wine. The honest competition is not Headspace; it's escapism."

## The brand truth

[The thing that's true about this product that isn't true about competitors. If you can't write this in one sentence, the brand isn't clear yet.]

Example: "We respect the user's existing practice. We are not introducing them to meditation; we are sitting with them while they do theirs."

## The single feeling

[The one word someone should leave with. Force the choice.]

Example: "Quiet."

---

# Position

## What this design IS

[The anchor. A metaphor, a lineage, a material, a constraint. State it in one sentence.]

Example: "This design is a paper bookmark — small, considered, present without demanding attention."

## What this design IS NOT

[Three to five things you reject. Be specific. Naming what you're not is how you sharpen what you are.]

Example:

- Not a wellness app (no smoothie colors, no smiling faces).
- Not a productivity tool (no streaks, no "you've meditated 7 days in a row").
- Not Headspace (no animation, no narrator persona, no cartoon).
- Not minimalist for minimalism's sake (it's quiet because the user is, not because we're stylish).

## The lineage we draw on

[The design tradition this rests on. From `aesthetic-lineages.md` or your own. Name it and explain why it fits.]

Example: "Japanese editorial / stationery. Specifically: Hara's Muji-era writing on emptiness as content, the design of fountain pen boxes, traditional bookmark design. We draw on this tradition because the brief asks for quiet attentiveness; this lineage has spent centuries on that exact problem."

## The unforgettable thing

[One detail, mark, or moment that will be remembered. Specific.]

Example: "The session timer is a single horizontal line that recedes from full width to zero. No numbers. The line itself is the time. When the session ends, the line is gone."
```

---

## Notes on filling this out

**On specificity.** "A busy professional" is not a person. "A pediatric nurse on a 12-hour shift, trying to chart between patients" is a person. Specificity is what makes briefs useful.

**On the entry/exit states.** Two words is harder than it sounds. The discipline of choosing them is the value. If you have three states, you have two products.

**On "what this replaces."** Most product briefs name the wrong competition. The honest competition is usually a non-software activity. Naming it forces honesty.

**On the brand truth.** The truth is what survives a hostile critic. If your brand truth would be true of a competitor, it's not specific enough.

**On "what this is not."** This is the most-skipped section and the most valuable. Rejection is what gives the position spine. A position with no rejections is a vibe, not a position.

**On the unforgettable thing.** It's a single detail, not a paragraph of vibes. Force the choice. The discipline of picking one is the value.

---

## What to do with the finished brief

1. Show it to the user. Read it back. Ask: "Is this what you meant?"
2. Iterate on the brief, not on the design. Fixing a misaligned brief is cheap; fixing a misaligned design is expensive.
3. Once approved, treat the brief as the source of truth for every subsequent decision. When stuck, return to it. The position is the answer.
